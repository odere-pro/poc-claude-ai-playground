# GREEN Risk Examples — Dutch Labour Law (Migrant Worker Focus)

## Purpose

This file contains reference examples for **GREEN-level risk classification** under Dutch labour law, written from the perspective of a **staffing agency compliance team**.

**GREEN means:** The agency's practice or contract clause is legally compliant in the context of migrant worker employment. No violation is present or imminent. The scenario demonstrates correct application of Dutch labour law, appropriate documentation, and fair treatment of the worker. No corrective action is required, though good-practice notes may highlight ways to maintain compliance going forward.

Primary audience: Staffing agency compliance teams assessing contracts and practices involving migrant workers in agriculture, logistics, hospitality, and construction.

---

### Example 1: Bilingual Contract with Confirmed Understanding
**Sector:** All sectors
**Scenario:** A Romanian worker is hired by a logistics agency. The agency provides the employment contract in both Dutch and Romanian. The worker is given two days to read it, ask questions, and take it home. Before signing, the worker signs a separate acknowledgement confirming they received the contract in their language, understood the key terms, and had the opportunity to seek clarification. The Dutch version is the legally binding version; the Romanian version is the working translation.
**Why GREEN:** This practice fully satisfies the Dutch requirement to provide employment information in a language the worker understands, and goes further by documenting the worker's understanding. The signed acknowledgement protects the agency from any future claim that the worker did not understand restrictive clauses (non-compete, training repayment, probation). The process is documented and auditable.
**Good Practice Note:** Keep the signed acknowledgements in worker files for the duration of employment plus 5 years. If the agency uses multiple languages across its workforce, maintain translation accuracy by using certified translators rather than internal staff.

### Example 2: Housing Deduction Properly Separated and Documented
**Sector:** Agriculture / Construction
**Scenario:** An agricultural agency provides tied housing to Polish seasonal workers. The housing arrangement is documented as a separate written agreement from the employment contract, specifying: monthly rent of €200, what is included (utilities, wifi), the notice period to vacate (one month), and that the housing arrangement continues independently of employment status. After deducting rent, the worker's net effective hourly pay is €15.20 — above the statutory minimum wage of €14.40.
**Why GREEN:** Housing and employment are correctly separated into two distinct agreements. The deduction amount does not push net pay below minimum wage. The worker has clarity on what they are paying for, what it includes, and what happens to their housing if employment ends. The arrangement cannot be used as a coercive tool because the housing contract has its own independent notice period.
**Good Practice Note:** Annually verify that the total deduction amount (housing plus any transport) continues to leave net wages above the minimum wage floor, particularly after the January minimum wage indexation. Update housing contract terms if costs change rather than adjusting deductions unilaterally.

### Example 3: ABU CAO Phase Progression Correctly Managed
**Sector:** Logistics / Hospitality
**Scenario:** A Filipino worker has been placed by an agency at a distribution centre. The agency's HR system tracks phase progression automatically. At 26 weeks (end of Phase A), the agency assessed the comparable direct-hire wage at the client site and adjusted the worker's pay to match. At week 78, the agency offered the worker a Phase C permanent contract. The worker accepted. All phase transitions were communicated in writing, in the worker's preferred language, at least four weeks before each transition date.
**Why GREEN:** The agency followed the ABU CAO phase system precisely. Equal treatment was applied at the correct threshold (26 weeks), not deferred or ignored. The permanent contract offer was made at the statutory moment (78 weeks), not after — meaning the agency was never in breach. The written advance communications give the worker time to plan and demonstrate the agency is acting in good faith.
**Good Practice Note:** When offering the Phase C permanent contract, confirm the comparable wage is still accurate — direct hire rates at the client may have changed between week 26 and week 78. A small recalibration at this stage prevents a future underpayment claim.

### Example 4: Piece-Rate Pay With Guaranteed Minimum Wage Floor
**Sector:** Agriculture
**Scenario:** A horticultural agency pays workers on a piece-rate basis for tomato picking (€0.09 per kg). However, the contract also includes an explicit guarantee: "Regardless of output, the worker will receive a minimum of €14.40 per hour for all hours worked." The agency tracks hours worked alongside output. In weeks where piece-rate earnings exceed the minimum wage floor, workers earn the higher amount. In weeks where they fall short, the minimum wage guarantee is applied automatically.
**Why GREEN:** This structure fully complies with Dutch minimum wage law. The piece-rate creates genuine earnings upside without exposing the worker — or the agency — to below-minimum-wage outcomes. The explicit contractual guarantee removes ambiguity and provides the agency with a clear, auditable compliance mechanism.
**Good Practice Note:** Ensure that time tracking for piece-rate workers is accurate and independently verifiable (e.g., clock-in/clock-out records). If the Labour Inspectorate audits the arrangement, the agency will need to demonstrate that the minimum floor was applied correctly in every pay period, not just on average.

### Example 5: Zero-Hour Contract Converted to Minimum-Hours Contract at the Right Time
**Sector:** Hospitality / Cleaning
**Scenario:** A Vietnamese worker was hired on a zero-hour contract for a hotel cleaning agency. After four months, the worker's hours had been consistently 24–28 hours per week. The agency proactively offered the worker a minimum-hours contract guaranteeing 24 hours/week, which the worker accepted. The conversion was documented in a written contract addendum. The worker's terms otherwise remained the same.
**Why GREEN:** The agency correctly identified the emerging implied-hours risk and resolved it proactively. By formalising the minimum-hours obligation before any dispute arose, the agency both protected the worker and eliminated the uncertainty of an implied contract claim. The worker receives better job security; the agency has clear, documented obligations.
**Good Practice Note:** When converting zero-hour workers to minimum-hours contracts, set the guaranteed minimum slightly below the worker's actual average hours to retain some scheduling flexibility. A worker averaging 26 hours guaranteed at 24 is more sustainable than a guarantee of 26 that can't flex during quiet periods.

### Example 6: Correct Identification and Application of Sector CAO
**Sector:** Construction
**Scenario:** A Polish construction worker is placed at a client site. Before drafting the contract, the agency's compliance team identifies that the client falls under the Bouw CAO (construction sector collective agreement), which provides higher overtime rates and additional tools allowances than the generic ABU CAO. The contract explicitly names the Bouw CAO as the governing agreement. The worker is paid the Bouw CAO overtime premium (150% on weekdays, 175% on Saturdays) and receives the tools allowance.
**Why GREEN:** The agency correctly applied sector-specific CAO rules rather than defaulting to the lower ABU CAO rates. The worker is named in the contract, knows which CAO governs their terms, and is paid correctly. The agency has no underpayment exposure for this worker.
**Good Practice Note:** Document the reasoning for the CAO selection in the worker's file. If the client changes sector classification or the placement scope changes (e.g., from construction to maintenance), reassess which CAO applies. CAO misapplication in the other direction — applying a higher CAO than required — can also create issues with client invoicing.

### Example 7: Working Hours Compliant Including Travel Time for Site Workers
**Sector:** Construction / Agriculture
**Scenario:** The agency places construction workers across multiple sites. The contract correctly reflects variable work locations. The agency has obtained a legal opinion confirming that for these workers, travel from a designated morning assembly point to sites constitutes working time. Work schedules are capped at 10 hours/day including travel. No worker exceeds 50 hours/week on average. 11-hour rest periods between shifts are maintained.
**Why GREEN:** The agency has proactively addressed the travel-time question rather than ignoring it. By including travel in working time calculations, the agency's schedules comply with the Arbeidstijdenwet even under the stricter interpretation. The conservative 10-hour cap provides buffer below the 12-hour absolute daily maximum.
**Good Practice Note:** Document the travel time policy and ensure client site managers understand it. Construction clients sometimes informally request workers "just a bit earlier" — the agency should have a clear protocol that site managers are not permitted to extend working time beyond the schedule.

### Example 8: Transparent Off-Boarding With All Final Entitlements Paid
**Sector:** All sectors
**Scenario:** A Bulgarian worker's fixed-term contract with a logistics agency expires after 12 months. The agency provides the worker with: (1) a written statement confirming the contract end date and reason; (2) a final payslip in the worker's language showing accrued but unused holiday days and the cash payout for them; (3) the correct pro-rated holiday allowance (8%) for the months worked; (4) a reference letter; and (5) information in Bulgarian about how to claim unemployment benefits from the UWV. The worker is paid all final amounts on their last working day.
**Why GREEN:** Every legal obligation at end of employment is met and documented: final payslip, unused holiday cash-out, holiday allowance, and timely final payment. The reference letter and UWV information go beyond the legal minimum but reflect good practice in the migrant worker context — workers who do not know their post-employment rights are more likely to pursue claims against the agency later.
**Good Practice Note:** Create a standardised off-boarding checklist for all workers, available in the main languages of the workforce. Consistent application of this checklist protects the agency from post-employment claims and builds the trust that leads to workers returning for the next seasonal placement.
