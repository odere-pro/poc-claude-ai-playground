# AMBER Risk Examples — Dutch Labour Law (Migrant Worker Focus)

## Purpose

This file contains reference examples for **AMBER-level risk classification** under Dutch labour law, written from the perspective of a **staffing agency compliance team**.

**AMBER means:** A potential legal violation exists, or the situation is in a grey zone where a violation is likely if no corrective action is taken. The facts are ambiguous, a legal threshold is being approached, or a practice is compliant in form but risky in substance. Review is required. No immediate enforcement action has occurred, but the agency faces real exposure if the situation is not addressed.

Primary audience: Staffing agency compliance teams assessing contracts and practices involving migrant workers in agriculture, logistics, hospitality, and construction.

---

### Example 1: Housing Deductions Within Permitted Limits — But Not Transparently Documented
**Sector:** Agriculture / Construction
**Scenario:** An agency deducts €240/month for worker housing from gross wages. After deduction, the worker's net pay is €14.60/hour — just above the statutory minimum wage. The deduction is mentioned briefly in the contract as "accommodation costs may apply" but the exact amount, calculation method, and the housing contract itself are never provided separately to the worker.
**Why AMBER:** The deduction amount may be legally permissible if the housing is genuine, the costs are reasonable, and net pay remains above minimum wage. However, the lack of a separate, transparent housing contract is a risk factor. If the worker challenges the deduction, the absence of a clearly documented agreement weakens the agency's position significantly. Courts expect housing and employment to be treated as separate contractual relationships.
**Agency Risk:** If challenged, the deduction may be deemed unauthorised due to lack of proper documentation. Depending on audit timing, a Labour Inspectorate review could find this borderline. Aggregated across a large worker cohort, even small documentation gaps create meaningful liability.
**Action Required:** Issue a separate written housing contract to every worker in tied accommodation, specifying the monthly cost, what it covers, and the termination terms for the housing arrangement. Ensure net wages after all deductions remain above minimum wage in every pay period, not just on average.

### Example 2: Contract References "Applicable CAO" Without Specifying Which One
**Sector:** Logistics / Hospitality
**Scenario:** A staffing agency's standard contract template includes the phrase: "The applicable Collective Labour Agreement (CAO) governs terms not specified herein." No CAO is named. Several workers placed at a logistics firm are entitled to the Transport CAO, which provides higher overtime rates and additional rest day provisions than the generic ABU CAO.
**Why AMBER:** The vague reference to "applicable CAO" does not satisfy the employer's obligation to inform workers which CAO governs their employment — a mandatory disclosure under Dutch law. If the higher-value Transport CAO applies and was not disclosed, the agency may owe arrears for unpaid overtime premiums and rest day entitlements. The ambiguity also makes it easier for workers to argue that the more favourable interpretation of any disputed term applies.
**Agency Risk:** Retroactive claims for the difference between ABU CAO rates and the correct sector-specific CAO rates, potentially across many workers over an extended period. Regulatory finding of inadequate worker information.
**Action Required:** Identify the correct applicable CAO for each placement (determined by the client's sector, not the agency's). Name it explicitly in all contracts. Audit whether workers in the affected placements are owed arrears under the correct CAO.

### Example 3: On-Call Workers Developing a Consistent Hours Pattern
**Sector:** Hospitality / Agriculture
**Scenario:** A group of Bulgarian workers are employed on zero-hour (oproepcontract) contracts at a food processing facility. Over 11 months, all of them have worked between 30–36 hours per week consistently, every week. The agency continues treating them as on-call workers with no guaranteed hours.
**Why AMBER:** Under Dutch law, if an on-call worker consistently works a regular number of hours over an extended period, a court can find that a minimum-hours obligation has been implied — equal to their historical average. At 11 months of consistent 30–36 hour weeks, that argument is credible and strengthening every week. A worker who is suddenly given fewer hours can file a claim for the implied minimum contract hours.
**Agency Risk:** If any worker is offered reduced shifts, they can pursue a claim for the shortfall against their implied minimum of ~30 hours/week. The longer the pattern continues without formalisation, the harder it becomes to reduce hours without liability.
**Action Required:** Review the hours history for all on-call workers. For workers who have worked a consistent pattern for more than 3 months, consider converting them to a minimum-hours contract that reflects their actual average. This reduces dispute risk and is more transparent for the worker.

### Example 4: Fixed-Term Contract Renewals Not Centrally Tracked
**Sector:** All sectors
**Scenario:** The agency has 80 active migrant worker placements. Contract renewals are managed at individual account manager level without a central tracking system. An internal audit finds that at least 12 workers have been on consecutive fixed-term contracts for 28–32 months without any review of whether they are approaching the 36-month conversion threshold.
**Why AMBER:** Workers at 28–32 months are within 4–8 months of automatic conversion to a permanent contract. This is not yet a violation — no worker has passed the threshold. But without a tracking system, the agency may inadvertently issue a renewal that causes a conversion without realising it, or worse, attempt to end a relationship as a fixed-term expiry when the worker has already legally become a permanent employee.
**Agency Risk:** Unintended permanent employment obligations. If the agency attempts to "not renew" a contract that has already converted, this is an invalid termination of a permanent employee — triggering severance liability and wrongful dismissal exposure.
**Action Required:** Implement a central contract duration tracker with automated alerts at 30 and 34 months. For each of the 12 flagged workers, decide now whether to offer a permanent contract or end the relationship before the threshold is crossed. Do not issue further fixed-term renewals without a legal review.

### Example 5: Payslips Issued in Dutch Only — Workers Cannot Read Them
**Sector:** All sectors
**Scenario:** The agency issues standardised Dutch-language payslips to all workers, including 40+ who speak only Romanian, Polish, or Portuguese. Workers regularly approach the agency's front desk asking to have deductions explained verbally. The agency has not received formal complaints.
**Why AMBER:** Payslips must be comprehensible to the worker receiving them. While there is no explicit statutory requirement that payslips be in the worker's language, the practical effect of an incomprehensible payslip is that the worker cannot verify their pay — which undermines their ability to identify underpayment or illegal deductions. Courts and the Labour Inspectorate regard systematic comprehension failures as a compliance indicator. The absence of formal complaints does not mean workers understand their pay.
**Agency Risk:** If underpayment or illegal deductions are later discovered, the agency cannot rely on "the worker didn't object" as a defence when the payslip was in a language they could not read. Systemic incomprehension also makes wage theft investigations more damaging, as it suggests the agency relied on workers not being able to check their pay.
**Action Required:** Provide payslips with key fields translated, or provide a bilingual payslip template. At minimum, offer workers a one-time payslip explanation session in their language and document it. For the agency's own protection, ensure workers can verify what they are being paid.

### Example 6: Training Repayment Clause With No Proportionality Cap
**Sector:** Construction / Logistics
**Scenario:** A construction agency's contract includes the following clause: "If the worker leaves the agency within 24 months of completing any training provided by the agency, the worker will repay 100% of training costs." A worker who completed a €1,200 safety certification course is now leaving after 11 months.
**Why AMBER:** Training repayment clauses are conditionally permitted under Dutch law, but they must be proportional. A flat 100% repayment with a 24-month cliff is unlikely to survive a legal challenge. Courts expect a sliding scale that reduces the repayment obligation as time passes (e.g., 100% in month 1, decreasing to 0% by month 24). A flat repayment is especially problematic for migrant workers on short-term placements where a 24-month commitment is structurally difficult.
**Agency Risk:** The clause may be unenforceable as written, meaning the agency cannot recover the training cost. If the agency attempts to deduct the repayment from the worker's final wages, this is a prohibited deduction — creating additional liability. The clause also creates reputational risk if workers feel penalised for mobility.
**Action Required:** Revise all training repayment clauses to include a proportional sliding scale. Do not deduct repayment amounts from final wages without explicit written agreement. Legal review recommended before attempting to enforce any existing repayment claims.

### Example 7: Agency Worker Approaching 78-Week Permanent Contract Entitlement — No Plan in Place
**Sector:** Logistics / Hospitality
**Scenario:** Fourteen agency workers placed at a distribution centre have been in continuous placement for between 65 and 74 weeks. The agency has no process for managing the ABU CAO phase transition to Phase C (permanent contract entitlement at 78 weeks). Account managers are unaware of the specific workers' tenure.
**Why AMBER:** At 78 weeks of continuous placement, workers are entitled to a direct permanent employment contract under the ABU CAO. This is not optional or at the agency's discretion. Workers reaching this threshold without being offered a contract can file a claim for permanent employment, back-dated to when the entitlement arose. At 65–74 weeks, the agency has between 4 and 13 weeks to make a decision.
**Agency Risk:** If workers reach 78 weeks without a contract offer, the agency is in breach of the ABU CAO. Workers can pursue the permanent contract entitlement, potentially including back-dated employment protections and severance obligations.
**Action Required:** Immediately audit all placements against the ABU phase timeline. For workers approaching 78 weeks, decide within the next two weeks whether to offer permanent contracts, end placements before 78 weeks with proper notice, or seek a direct employment arrangement with the client. Do not let any worker cross the threshold without a formal decision.

### Example 8: Contract Lacks Specification of Work Location for Mobile/Site-Based Workers
**Sector:** Construction / Agriculture
**Scenario:** Construction workers' contracts state their place of work as "the agency's registered office in Amsterdam." In practice, workers are sent to different sites across the Netherlands, sometimes requiring 90-minute commutes each way. The contract does not specify how travel time is handled or whether it constitutes working time.
**Why AMBER:** For workers without a fixed workplace, Dutch law requires the contract to indicate that work locations are variable and specify how this is managed. More critically, travel to and from client sites (not the worker's home) can constitute working time under Dutch and EU law — particularly for workers who have no fixed office. If travel time is working time and is not being compensated, minimum wage and working hours violations may follow.
**Agency Risk:** If workers' travel time counts as working time, daily hour maximums may already be exceeded, and minimum wage calculations may need to account for the additional hours. This is a live legal debate in Dutch courts and the direction of case law favours workers. Proactive clarification now is significantly cheaper than retroactive liability.
**Action Required:** Amend contracts to accurately reflect variable work locations. Assess whether travel between sites constitutes working time for these workers and document the legal reasoning. Review whether daily hour limits are being exceeded when travel is included. Obtain legal advice on current Dutch case law regarding travel time for construction workers.
