# RED Risk Examples — Dutch Labour Law (Migrant Worker Focus)

## Purpose

This file contains reference examples for **RED-level risk classification** under Dutch labour law, written from the perspective of a **staffing agency compliance team**.

**RED means:** A clear, existing legal violation. The agency faces immediate exposure to regulatory fines, back-pay liability, court orders, or reputational damage. These situations require urgent corrective action — not further investigation. The breach is evident on the face of the contract or practice.

Primary audience: Staffing agency compliance teams assessing contracts and practices involving migrant workers in agriculture, logistics, hospitality, and construction.

---

### Example 1: Housing Deductions Pushing Wages Below Minimum
**Sector:** Agriculture / Logistics
**Scenario:** A Polish seasonal worker earns €14.40/hour (gross minimum wage). The agency deducts €350/month for accommodation and €80/month for transport to the farm from the worker's net salary. The net result is effective hourly compensation of approximately €11.80 — well below the statutory minimum wage floor.
**Why RED:** Dutch law requires minimum wage to be paid as net cash in the worker's hand after all deductions. Housing and transport deductions that push effective pay below the minimum wage are illegal regardless of whether the worker "agreed" to them in the contract. The agency is liable for the full shortfall for every month this arrangement has been in place.
**Agency Liability:** Back-pay for all months below minimum wage, plus statutory interest. Potential Labour Inspectorate fine (up to €10,000 per violation per worker). Regulatory scrutiny of the agency's entire worker portfolio.
**Action Required:** Immediately recalculate all affected workers' net pay after deductions. Issue back-pay for all shortfalls. Either raise gross wages to absorb the deductions or reduce deduction amounts. Review all contracts where tied housing arrangements exist. Voluntarily disclose to the Labour Inspectorate to reduce fine severity.

### Example 2: Employment Contract Provided Only in Dutch to Non-Dutch Speaker
**Sector:** All sectors
**Scenario:** A Romanian worker is hired by an agricultural staffing agency. The employment contract is written entirely in Dutch. The worker does not speak Dutch. The agency's practice is to hand over the contract and ask the worker to sign on the spot. No translation or explanation is provided.
**Why RED:** Dutch law requires employment information to be provided in a language the employee understands. A contract the worker cannot read is not a valid basis for the terms within it. Any clause that disadvantages the worker — a non-compete, a training repayment clause, a probation waiver, reduced notice — cannot be enforced. Beyond enforceability, this practice exposes the agency to claims of misrepresentation and potentially fraud.
**Agency Liability:** Unenforceability of restrictive clauses. If terms were materially misrepresented, courts may void the entire contract. Regulatory risk if the practice is systemic. High litigation exposure — workers successfully challenge terms they demonstrably could not have understood.
**Action Required:** Provide contracts in both Dutch and the worker's primary language (or a certified translation). Implement a sign-off process confirming the worker received, read, and understood the contract. Audit all existing contracts to identify workers who signed Dutch-only agreements. Do not rely on oral explanations as a substitute for written translation.

### Example 3: Workers Registered as Self-Employed (ZZP) Against All Indicators
**Sector:** Construction / Logistics
**Scenario:** A construction company uses an agency to place 15 Eastern European workers. All are registered as ZZP (self-employed contractors). In practice: the agency sets their daily rates and working hours, they use the client's tools and equipment, they cannot refuse work without losing their placement, they have worked exclusively for this client for 14 months, and they cannot substitute or delegate tasks.
**Why RED:** All primary indicators of false self-employment (schijnzelfstandigheid) are present. Dutch courts and the tax authority (Belastingdienst) assess the totality of the working relationship, not the label. This arrangement is a clear case of disguised employment. The agency and client are jointly exposed for unpaid employer social security contributions, back-payment of employment entitlements (severance, holiday pay, sick pay), and potentially back-taxes.
**Agency Liability:** Full employer social security contributions retroactively owed for all workers for the entire engagement period. All employment entitlements owed as though formal contracts existed. Potential fraud investigation if the agency was aware the arrangement was non-compliant. NBBU/ABU certification risk if the agency holds a quality mark.
**Action Required:** Immediately convert affected workers to formal employment or genuine sub-contractor arrangements that meet all self-employment criteria. Engage a tax/legal specialist to calculate the back-contribution liability. Report to Belastingdienst to access voluntary disclosure protocols and reduce penalties.

### Example 4: Illegal Wage Deductions — "Recruitment Fees" and "Administration Costs"
**Sector:** Agriculture / Hospitality
**Scenario:** A hospitality staffing agency deducts €150/month per worker as a "recruitment fee" and €25/month as a "payroll administration fee." These deductions are stated in the contract. Workers are primarily from Indonesia and the Philippines. The deductions have been applied consistently for two years.
**Why RED:** Dutch law prohibits employers from charging workers for the cost of recruitment, agency fees, or payroll administration. These are employer business costs, not employee obligations. The deductions are illegal regardless of whether they appear in a signed contract — an employee cannot validly consent to an illegal deduction. These are among the most common forms of wage theft in the migrant worker sector.
**Agency Liability:** Full refund of all deductions ever taken, across all affected workers, plus statutory interest. Labour Inspectorate fine. If the pattern is systemic, potential criminal referral for labour exploitation. Reputational risk with NGO and media exposure — this type of violation is regularly publicised.
**Action Required:** Stop all deductions immediately. Calculate and repay the full amount extracted from every affected worker. Review the entire fee and deduction structure across the agency's worker portfolio. Engage legal counsel to assess criminal exposure given the duration of the practice.

### Example 5: Tied Housing Clause Linking Job Loss to Immediate Eviction
**Sector:** Agriculture / Construction
**Scenario:** A seasonal agricultural worker's contract includes a clause stating: "Termination of this employment contract automatically terminates the accommodation agreement, with the worker required to vacate within 24 hours." The worker lives in agency-provided housing and has no other address in the Netherlands.
**Why RED:** This clause creates coercive control over the worker. Housing and employment are legally separate relationships and cannot be used as leverage against each other in this way. The clause is almost certainly unenforceable as written — 24-hour eviction notice violates tenancy law regardless of any contract. More critically, the clause is a textbook indicator of labour exploitation as defined under Dutch criminal law (Article 273f Criminal Code). The agency faces potential criminal liability, not just civil.
**Agency Liability:** Criminal exposure for labour exploitation (labour trafficking indicators). Civil liability for any harm caused to a worker evicted or threatened with eviction. Immediate regulatory scrutiny from the Dutch Labour Authority (NLA). Possible ABU/NBBU quality mark suspension.
**Action Required:** Remove the clause from all contracts immediately. Issue written confirmation to all current workers that housing is not conditional on continued employment. Conduct a full audit of housing contracts. Engage criminal law counsel immediately.

### Example 6: Working Hours Exceeding Legal Maximums in Harvest Season
**Sector:** Agriculture
**Scenario:** During peak harvest season, the agency instructs workers at a horticultural client to work 7 days/week, typically 13–14 hours/day. This has continued for six consecutive weeks. The agency is aware; the schedule was agreed with the client's site manager.
**Why RED:** The Arbeidstijdenwet caps daily working hours at 12 hours (absolute maximum) and weekly averages at 55 hours over 4 weeks and 48 hours over 16 weeks. Working 13–14 hours/day violates the daily cap every day. Working 7 days/week violates the mandatory 35-hour consecutive weekly rest requirement. The fact that the client requested it, or that workers accepted it, provides no legal defence. Emergency exceptions are narrow and temporary; six weeks is not an emergency.
**Agency Liability:** Labour Inspectorate fines (per worker, per violation day — these compound rapidly). Civil liability if workers suffer health harm. Potential invalidation of insurance coverage for workplace injuries during illegal hours. Joint liability with the client employer.
**Action Required:** Immediately cap schedules at 12 hours/day maximum and enforce the 35-hour weekly rest. Communicate this to the client in writing, noting the agency's legal obligations. If the client insists on non-compliant schedules, terminate the placement agreement. Begin calculating compensatory rest owed to current workers.

### Example 7: Agency Worker Equal Treatment Ignored Past 26 Weeks
**Sector:** Logistics / Hospitality
**Scenario:** A Bulgarian worker has been placed by an agency at the same logistics company for 18 months continuously. Under the ABU CAO, after 26 weeks (Phase A), agency workers are entitled to equal pay and conditions as directly employed workers doing the same role. The agency has not assessed comparator pay and continues paying the worker at Phase A rates well into month 18.
**Why RED:** The ABU CAO (the standard collective agreement for temp agencies) creates a clear phase progression. After 78 weeks, the worker is entitled to a permanent contract. The agency's failure to progress the worker to equal treatment has created 12+ months of underpayment. The worker can claim the difference retroactively, with statutory interest, for the entire period of non-compliance.
**Agency Liability:** Back-pay liability for the full underpayment period (potentially €3,000–€8,000+ per worker depending on the pay differential). Legal costs if the worker pursues a claim. Reputational risk with both worker networks and client companies. Risk of ABU certification review.
**Action Required:** Immediately assess the comparable direct-hire wage at the client site. Pay the correct phase rate from the date the worker qualified. Calculate and pay all arrears. Implement a systematic phase tracking process for all agency workers to prevent recurrence.

### Example 8: Piece-Rate Pay Falling Below Minimum Wage in Practice
**Sector:** Agriculture
**Scenario:** Workers at a flower-picking farm are paid on a piece-rate basis (€0.08 per bunch picked). The agency advertises this as "competitive earnings potential." In practice, average workers pick 120–150 bunches/hour. At 120 bunches, that is €9.60/hour — €4.80 below the statutory minimum wage of €14.40. This gap is not compensated by a base salary.
**Why RED:** Piece-rate pay must meet the minimum wage floor for every hour worked. It is not sufficient for a worker to theoretically earn minimum wage if they are highly productive — if the average or low-productivity worker earns below minimum wage, the agency is underpaying. The minimum wage applies to actual hours, not projected output. This is one of the most common minimum wage violations in the Dutch agricultural sector.
**Agency Liability:** Full back-pay for every hour every worker was paid below minimum wage, plus statutory interest. Labour Inspectorate fines. Joint liability with the agricultural client if the piece-rate structure was agreed between them.
**Action Required:** Introduce a guaranteed hourly floor of at least €14.40/hour regardless of output. Calculate and repay all minimum wage shortfalls. Renegotiate piece-rate arrangements with the client to allow lawful pay structures. Audit all piece-rate placements across the agency's portfolio.
